WiniCari — AI Layer for Tunisian Bus Operations
================================================

WHAT WE ARE DOING
-----------------
Adding an AI intelligence layer on top of the WiniCari bus-management platform
used by Tunisian bus companies (S.R.T.K, S.R.T.N, S.T.S). The platform already
stores GPS telemetry, ticketing records, and route definitions in MongoDB. We do
not touch that system — we read from it, build a clean dataset, and train models
on top.

WHY
---
Tunisian bus operators have no real-time intelligence: they cannot predict when a
bus will be late, they lose track of buses during GPS outages, they have no
automated way to flag broken or suspicious trips, and dispatchers have no easy
way to query fleet data in plain language. This project gives them all four
capabilities without requiring any change to their existing software.

THE DATA FOUNDATION
-------------------
Raw GPS pings and ticketing records are noisy and fragmented. Before any modeling
we reconstruct clean trips: match GPS positions to known stops, compute elapsed
times, dwell times at each stop, and distances. This produces a structured table
of 168,481 stop-level rows covering 20,743 trips across 29 lines (Jan 2025 to
Jun 2026). Everything downstream is built on this foundation.

THE 4 MODULES AND THEIR FEATURES
---------------------------------

Module 1 — Delay Prediction
  What: predicts how many minutes late a bus will be at every remaining stop,
        updated in real time as the trip progresses.
  Features used: current delay, elapsed time since departure, stop index,
                 fraction of route completed, hour of day, day of week,
                 weekend flag, line, direction.
  Models: HistGBM (MAE ~2.7 min), LSTM (sequence of past stops), Prophet
          (daily average delay forecast per line for planning).
  Why: passengers and dispatchers can see accurate ETAs instead of fixed
       timetables that rarely hold.

Module 2 — GPS Fallback
  What: estimates the bus position when the GPS signal drops (common in
        tunnels, dense areas, or transponder faults).
  Features used: last known position and speed, Kalman filter state
                 (position + velocity + uncertainty), recent speed history.
  Models: linear interpolation (short gaps), dead reckoning, Kalman filter,
          LSTM correction on top of Kalman (learns route-specific bias).
  Why: GPS outages currently create blind spots in the monitoring dashboard.
       The fallback keeps the bus visible with a growing uncertainty band
       until signal recovers.

Module 3 — Anomaly Detection
  What: automatically flags trips that look broken, unusual, or suspicious
        without any human labelling (fully unsupervised).
  Features used (trip-level): number of stops, GPS match rate, max and mean
        dwell time at a stop, total trip duration, max snap distance.
  Features used (sequence-level): dwell time, snap distance, and matched flag
        at each stop in order.
  Models: Isolation Forest (flags ~5% of trips at the trip level), LSTM
          Autoencoder (flags trips whose stop-by-stop profile was never seen
          during training — pinpoints where in the trip the anomaly occurred).
  Why: manual inspection of 20,000+ trips per period is impossible. Automated
       flagging lets staff investigate only the ~5% that look genuinely wrong
       (parked layovers counted as trips, GPS drift causing false stops, etc.).

Module 4 — RAG Chatbot
  What: lets a dispatcher ask plain-language questions about the fleet and get
        answers grounded in real data ("Which lines had the most anomalies last
        month?", "What is the average delay on line 209?").
  How it works: line summaries, company stats, and anomaly reports are embedded
        with sentence-transformers and stored in ChromaDB. A question retrieves
        the relevant documents and Llama 3 (via Groq) writes the answer.
  Why: non-technical staff cannot write database queries. A chatbot removes
       that barrier entirely.

KNOWN LIMITATIONS AND OPEN QUESTIONS
--------------------------------------
- No actual scheduled timetables in the data — delay is measured against a
  statistical baseline built from historical GPS data, not official schedules.
- Lines with zero geocoded stops (likely terminal-to-terminal direct runs)
  are not fully handled yet — need more analysis to decide if they count as
  full trips or should be treated differently.
- Weather, Ramadan, and summer seasonal features have not been added to the
  delay model yet — would likely improve accuracy on predictable seasonal spikes.
- The LSTM delay model trains on all lines together; a per-line bias correction
  (as Kalman does) could further reduce error on specific routes.
- Partial trips (bus starting mid-route, turning back early) and layovers
  splitting trips need more careful definition and handling in the foundation.
